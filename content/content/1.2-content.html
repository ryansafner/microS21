Overview

We are diving right into what is commonly called “Consumer Theory.” This is often the most “alien” material to students in economics: indifference curves, preferences

constrained optimization models.

If you are following the textbook, it goes straight into (a review of principles, really) supply & demand. We will ultimately get to supply and demand, but by the time we do later this semester, you will have a much richer understanding and appreciation for them, having understood how they represent the decisions by optimizing individuals as buyers and sellers.

What we turn to now, for the next few classes,

and our first application, how individuals choose. Our primary focus will be people as consumers - how do they choose to spend their money and how do those choices change? While we will focus extensively on consumers making purchasing choices, do not think this is the only use of this type of model! Today we will cover the constraints in the model.

Now that we are picking up the intensity and working with some models (many will be new to you at first), I recommend looking at the textbook readings for each class.

We are going a bit out of order in two ways. First, Goolsbee et al. start with a review of Supply and Demand in chapters 2-3, and don’t get to “Consumer Theory” (what we are starting now) until chapter 4. Second, they start with the “newer” and “weirder” stuff first — indifference curves and utility functions, and then teach budget constraints second. I will go in the opposite order to slowly acclimate you from familiar territory (linear equations) into newer tools and concepts (nonlinear functions, multivariate functions, optimization, etc). This is also a good time to review my Math Review Guide for next week.
